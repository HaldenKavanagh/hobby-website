The Walstead Method

How it works Materials

The walstead method is a natural way to set up your fishtank, without the need for water changes, regular cleaning, 02, C02, and eventually food. Aside from the cost of the tank and light, it can be done for free and requires little to no maintnence.

How does it work?

The most important part of a successfull Walstead tank is the bacteria, without it the tank would'nt function. It is because of this that the substrate is so important. By having a thin layer of soil at the bottom, and a deep layer of sand or gravel above it (atleast 2 inches), we can mimic the natural world to create a truly self sufficient ecosystem.

To understand this, you must know how the nitrogen cycle works.

The Nitrogen cycle and anaerobic denitrification

  • The Nitrogen cycle

    The Nitrogen cycle is basically the food chain for plants and animals in your tank. When things like leftover fish food, dead organisms, and waste products decompose they turn to ammonia. Ammonia is very toxic to your tak, and if it builds up, everything will die. The ammonia is then broken down by nitrosomas (ammonia eating bacteria) It turns into something less harmfull but still detremental to your tank, nitrites These nitrates are then broken down by by bacteria in the genus Nitrobacter into nitrates.

    At this point, a water change would be done to rid your tank of the nitrates, however this is not neccesarily needed. Thats where the walstead method comes in.

  • Anaerobic Nitrification

    Denitrification refers to the process where specific types of bacteria break down nitrates and nitrites into nitrogen gas. Denitrification only occurs anaerobically, meaning it only takes place where no oxygen is present. The bacteria that performs this is anaerobic, meaning they will die in contact with oxygen

    This is where the Walstead method's minimum two inches of sand comes to play. With a deep layer of sand, a portion of your tank exists where oxygen can penetrate, and thus creating an environment for denitrifying bacteria to live and complete the nitrogen cycle completely. This eliminates the need for water changes. It is very hard to get tis balance perfect, so expect to do mabie two 25% water changes a year

Materials

This method requires verry little investment, where the main cost is the tank and the light.

  • The Tank

    Any tank will do. As long as it holds water your good to go. Thrift stores are a viable option, as people often donate them in realy good condition.

  • Light

    Sometimes people will donate their light along with their tank, bu more than likely it will not sustain your tank and give the plants everything they need. When shopping for a uv light, make sure that it is a full spectrum grow light. Having one specifically for aquarium plants is preferable but not crucial.

  • Substrates

    This method uses a two-substrate method to ensure a layer where anaerobic bacteria can live. These layers include a thin, nutrient ritch layer of fertilizer capped by a thick layer of sand or fine gravel. for the first layer, you can buy expensive aquasoil and that works fine but for the budget route any old potting soil works great, just make sure to sift out all of the large debris beforehand. The same thing goes for the sand, you can go to the aquarium store and buy a big bag of ovepriced sand, or venture to your local river or lake to get some. Any sand will do, just try and get some cleanish sand or else it will take forever to clean. when you get home wash the sand thourally with water untill it runs clear. If you want to risk it, and you have some sand from a beutifull clean body of water you can attempt to skip the rinsing process but it is not recomended.

  • Decor

    while getting your sand, you may see a cool looking rock, or branch of a tree that you find interesting. Bring it back with you and we can use it to decorate your tank. search for cool rocks and pieces of wood with lots of texture and interest. Alternitively, you can go to the fish store and buy some fancy rocks and wood. If you collected your hardscape naturally, boil them. this will kill anything hiding in them and help to waterlog the wood. If your wood floats when submerged in water, soak it in warm water, using something heavy to keep it fully submerged. When it sinks naturally it is ready for your tank.

  • plants

    when buying plants for the first time, you dont need to go crazy. Later on when they start growing you can simply replant your trimmings to have infinite plants. Look for plants that peak your interest, and are visually appealing to you, Contrast is usually good but in general get what you think looks cool. Depending on the tank size, it is only neccesary to get two types of stem plants, and probably some carpeting or floating plants. The more plant growth the better, so dont be afraid to heavily plant your tank, but if you are on a budget you can allways just replant the trimmings to populate the tank.

  • Optional: Sponge filter

    A sponge filter is optional but can be an awesome addition to your tank. Buy a small oxygen pump and a sponge filter attatchment. connect the sponge filter to the air filter tube and put it in. It works by pushing O2 down the tube and into the filter. This release of gas creates a bit of a vacume that pulls the water into the sponge and filters it. You can get a pump for 15-20$ an the filter for around 10$.
